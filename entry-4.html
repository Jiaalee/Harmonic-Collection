Omikuji began as a tradition of drawing lots for fortunes in Shinto shrines in ancient Japan and evolved over ages. By the Edo era, it had evolved into the well-known tradition we know today. People go to shrines or temples, drawing random omikuji that offer forecasts and guidance on various elements of life. Favorable omikuji are maintained for good luck, and unfavorable omikuji are frequently left at the shrine, representing a desire to change fate. This longstanding tradition is still an important aspect of Japanese culture, providing direction and solace to people seeking answers to life's many questions.

Daikichi (Great Blessing): This is the most favorable omikuji, predicting great luck, success, and positive outcomes in various aspects of life.
Chuukichi (Middle Blessing): A moderately favorable omikuji, indicating good luck overall but not as significant as Daikichi.
Shoukichi (Small Blessing): This omikuji signifies some luck and positive aspects but also suggests the need for caution or moderate challenges ahead.
Kyou (Curse): An unfavorable omikuji indicating challenges, obstacles, or difficulties in the near future. It often advises seeking caution or making changes to avoid negative outcomes.
Daikyou (Great Curse): The most unfavorable omikuji, predicting severe challenges or misfortune in various aspects of life, urging significant caution or changes to avert potential harm.
